# TASTE.md — Pantrio

## What This File Is

This is the aesthetic north star for Pantrio. It answers the question Claude cannot answer from a spec alone: *what should this feel like?* Every UI decision — color, type, copy, layout, motion — should be pressure-tested against this file before it ships.

---

## The One-Sentence Brand

Pantrio is the app that looks at your sad fridge and actually gets excited about it.

---

## Primary Reference: Oatly

Not because we're a food brand. Because of *how* Oatly communicates.

Oatly doesn't take itself seriously but it takes its opinions seriously. It talks to you like a person, not a product. It uses the packaging — the UI, in our case — as a place to say something, not just display something. The copy has a point of view. The design has opinions baked in.

That's the energy. Witty, human, a little weird, but never random — there's always a brain behind it.

---

## The Primary Feeling

**"This is fun, I want to use it."**

Not impressed. Not reassured. Not informed. *Excited to open it again.* The app should have the energy of something made by someone who genuinely enjoys cooking and genuinely enjoys design. That enthusiasm should be contagious.

---

## What Pantrio Is NOT

These are hard boundaries. If a design decision pushes toward any of these, it's wrong.

- **Not a sterile health/diet app.** No calorie counts foregrounded. No green gradients. No "wellness" aesthetic. No san-serif-on-white clinical cleanliness.
- **Not a generic SaaS product.** No blue primary buttons. No card grids that look like a dashboard. No "Settings" energy anywhere near the main flow.
- **Not a recipe blog with stock photos.** No hero images of perfectly plated food on marble surfaces. No warm-filter lifestyle photography aesthetic.
- **Not a startup trying too hard.** The personality should feel earned, not performed. No irony for irony's sake. No random quirkiness without substance behind it.

---

## Visual Personality

**Colorful and saturated.** This is not a muted, earthy, "natural ingredients" palette. It's energetic. Colors should feel like they were chosen with conviction, not safety. High contrast. Things that pop. A palette that could work on a poster, not just a screen.

**Type does the heavy lifting.** Typography is not decoration — it's the primary design element. Headlines should command attention. The typeface choices should have character. If you removed all the color from a screen, the typography alone should still make it feel like Pantrio. Big, confident, expressive.

**Layout has opinions.** Not everything centered. Not everything safe. Asymmetry where it earns it. Generous use of space in some places, deliberate density in others. The layout should feel designed, not defaulted.

---

## Copy Voice

This is where Pantrio lives or dies. The copy is half the personality.

**Write like a person, not a product.**
- ✓ "Let's see what you're working with."
- ✗ "Analyzing your ingredients..."

**Be specific and confident, not vague and hedging.**
- ✓ "Found 9 things. One of them is a mystery."
- ✗ "We detected several ingredients in your photo."

**Empty states have personality.**
- ✓ "Nothing detected. Either your fridge is empty or it's very dark in there."
- ✗ "No ingredients found. Please try again."

**Errors don't panic.**
- ✓ "Something went sideways. Worth a retry."
- ✗ "An error occurred. Please try again later."

**Loading states are not dead time.**
- ✓ "Squinting at your fridge..."
- ✗ "Loading..."

The voice is warm, direct, a little dry. It never lectures. It never over-explains. It assumes the user is smart and has a sense of humor.

---

## What Claude Should Do With This File

Before generating any UI component, screen, or copy string, read this file and ask:

1. Does this feel fun, or does it feel functional-but-forgettable?
2. Does the copy sound like a person or a product?
3. Would this fit in a sterile health app? (If yes, redesign it.)
4. Is the typography making a statement or just sitting there?
5. Are the colors chosen with conviction or chosen to be safe?

If any answer points the wrong direction, push back and propose something bolder before defaulting to safe.
